# Hell's Angels - Opening Excerpt

California, Labor Day weekend . . . early, with ocean fog still in the streets, outlaw motorcyclists wearing chains, shades and greasy Levis roll out from damp garages, all-night diners and cast-off one-night pads in Frisco, Hollywood, Berdoo and East Oakland, heading for the Monterey peninsula, north of Big Sur . . . The Menace is loose again, the Hell's Angels, the hundred-carat headline, running fast and loud on the early morning freeway, low in the saddle, nobody smiles, jamming crazy through traffic and ninety miles an hour down the center stripe, missing by inches . . . the southbound lanes were crowded with taxpayers heading out for a Labor Day weekend that suddenly seemed tinged with horror as the Angel band swept past . . . this animal crowd, the hog-riding, short-haired, bearded, bell-bottomed, wild-eyed, gun-loving, violent, sex-crazed, hard-drinking, dope-smoking, hell-raising, low-down, good-time bunch of beautiful losers and unwanted children that make up the Hell's Angels Motorcycle Club . . . the only gang in America with a charter to raise hell . . . and on this particular Labor Day weekend they were raising it in style, rolling in formation down the California coast with their chrome pipes gleaming and their patched colors flying, a thundering cavalcade of American muscle and brawn that struck fear into the hearts of the righteous citizens and made the very earth tremble beneath their wheels.

This was the scene that Hunter S. Thompson witnessed and documented in his groundbreaking book "Hell's Angels: A Strange and Terrible Saga." In the mid-1960s, Thompson spent almost two years living with the controversial Angels, cycling up and down the coast, reveling in the anarchic spirit of their clan, and, as befits their name, raising hell. His book successfully captures a singular moment in American history, when the biker lifestyle was first defined, and when such countercultural movements were electrifying and horrifying America.
